Our successful recruitment of the heroes had earned me a bit of good will with Coil. I chose to spend it by requesting time with Dinah. I needed to be sure she was okay.

I stood before the room assigned to Dinah as her bedroom. A pair of armed guards were positioned on either side of the door. I would have loved to believe that they were there to protect Dinah, but I knew that they were in fact there to prevent the emaciated child from escaping. The guards opened the door for me and I stepped inside.

The room wasn't a cell, but it may as well have been. It was a drab gray without decoration or furnishings of any kind beyond the cot that was her bed.

Dinah lay on the bed, dead to the world. If it weren't for the slight movement of her tiny chest as she breathed, I would have thought she were an actual corpse. Her hair was dirty and fragile looking. Her skin was ashen and her eyes were sunken. She looked like a homeless drug addict, not a twelve-year-old from a wealthy family.

I took a seat on the stool beside her cot. "Dinah?" I called to her softly.

Her eyes remained closed but she moaned in acknowledgement.

"Hey, Dinah. It's me, Skitter." I reached out and placed my hand atop hers. She flinched but didn't reject me. "I just wanted to make sure you're okay."

"My head hurts," she mumbled.

I nodded, then realized that she couldn't see me. "I know. Coil has been working you too hard." The words came out harsh and bitter. I closed my eyes and took in a breath, trying to calm myself for the child. "I'm going to get you out of here, okay? I have an agreement with Coil. He promised to let you go if I can make myself as useful to him as you are."

Dinah turned her head toward me and opened her eyes. She looked like she immediately regretted it as she squinted to shield herself the overhead light.

"He won't let me go," she said with conviction.

I shook my head. "No. I'll make sure that he does."

"Skitter," Dinah said, looking upon me with pity. "There is a five point nine three percent chance that Coil does as he's told you. He won't let me go."

I was silent while I considered the implications. Dinah's predictions were never wrong. She couldn't see the future, exactly, just the probability of a certain result based on factored conditions across all potential futures. A 5.93% chance was not very promising. I never actually trusted Coil to uphold his end of the deal, but to have it spelled out in black and white like that was disheartening.

I gripped her hand in mine. "I'll find another way, then. We have the heroes on our side for the moment. I'll find another way."

Dinah looked at me again and winced, the same pained expression she always had when she ran the numbers.

"I believe you," she said, merely a whisper.
